\title{\textbf{A Consistency-Gated Quaternion Error-State Kalman Filter\\ for Multi-Sensor Inertial Navigation}}
\author{Irfan Saric}
\date{December 2025}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We describe the design, implementation, and verification of an error-state Kalman filter (ESKF)
that fuses a strapdown inertial measurement unit (IMU) with GPS, a barometric altimeter, a
magnetometer, a downward laser altimeter (LiDAR), ultra-wideband (UWB) radio ranging, and optical
flow to estimate the pose of an aerial vehicle. The filter follows the multiplicative, local
error-state formulation of Sol\`a~\cite{sola2017} and Trawny and Roumeliotis~\cite{trawny2005}: a
nominal state carries the full estimate and is integrated directly from the IMU, while a minimal
$15$-dimensional error state, reset to zero after every correction, carries the covariance. Each
measurement model is given with its exact error-state Jacobian, and every Jacobian is validated
against a finite difference. The central claim is not merely that the filter tracks well but that
its \emph{reported uncertainty is correct}: we verify this with the normalized estimation error
squared (NEES) consistency test of Bar-Shalom~et~al.~\cite{barshalom2001}, run as a Monte-Carlo gate
over many simulated flights. On the full seven-sensor problem the filter is statistically
consistent (position NEES $2.84$ against an expected $3.0$; full-state NEES $15.6$ against $15.0$),
while remaining faithful to inertial-navigation error theory: with all aiding removed, the position
error grows without bound, and it is recovered to sub-metre accuracy when a single aiding modality,
including UWB ranging alone, is restored.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Aided inertial navigation estimates a vehicle's position, velocity, and orientation by integrating
high-rate inertial measurements and correcting the accumulated drift with lower-rate aiding sensors
\cite{farrell2008,groves2013}. Orientation makes the problem subtle: a unit quaternion has four
components but only three degrees of freedom, so a filter that estimates it directly must either
enforce the unit-norm constraint or carry a covariance for a quantity that is not free to move in
four directions. The \emph{error-state} (or indirect, or multiplicative) Kalman filter resolves this
by estimating a minimal three-parameter rotation error in the tangent space of the current attitude
estimate~\cite{markley2003,sola2017}. This paper documents such a filter, its six measurement
models, and---most importantly---the statistical test that proves its covariance honest.

The contribution is one of rigor and reproducibility rather than novelty of formulation: the filter
is a faithful implementation of the standard local-error ESKF, but each Jacobian is
finite-difference-checked, the whole estimation loop is gated on NEES consistency, and the entire
system---core, simulator, and a real-time three-dimensional visualization---runs in the browser via
WebAssembly and WebGL2. Source and an interactive demonstration are available at
\url{https://github.com/Zarathos94/ekf-sim} and \url{https://irfansaric.com/showcase/ekf-sim}.

\section{State Representation}

The \textbf{nominal state} collects position $\bp\in\mathbb{R}^3$, velocity $\bv\in\mathbb{R}^3$,
orientation as a Hamilton unit quaternion $\bq\in\mathbb{S}^3$ (body to world), and the
accelerometer and gyroscope biases $\ba_b,\bomega_b\in\mathbb{R}^3$:
\begin{equation}
  \bx = (\bp,\ \bv,\ \bq,\ \ba_b,\ \bomega_b).
\end{equation}
The \textbf{error state} is the $15$-vector
\begin{equation}
  \dx = (\delta\bp,\ \delta\bv,\ \dtheta,\ \delta\ba_b,\ \delta\bomega_b) \in \mathbb{R}^{15},
\end{equation}
where the rotation error $\dtheta$ is defined \emph{locally} (in the body frame) through the
multiplicative relation
\begin{equation}
  \bq_{\text{true}} = \bq \otimes \exp\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\dtheta\right),
  \label{eq:localerror}
\end{equation}
with $\exp(\cdot)$ the quaternion exponential map and $\otimes$ the Hamilton product. The nominal
state holds the full, unconstrained estimate; the error state, kept near zero, holds a covariance
$\bP\in\mathbb{R}^{15\times15}$ whose orientation block is an ordinary $3\times3$ matrix rather than
a constrained $4\times4$ one. We write $\R=\R(\bq)$ for the rotation matrix and
$\skewm{\cdot}:\mathbb{R}^3\to\mathfrak{so}(3)$ for the skew-symmetric ``cross-product'' operator,
so that $\skewm{\ba}\bv = \ba\times\bv$.

\section{Propagation}

At each IMU sample (rate $\approx\SI{200}{\hertz}$, interval $\Delta t$) with accelerometer reading
$\ba_m$ and gyroscope reading $\bomega_m$, define the bias-corrected specific force and body rate
\begin{equation}
  \ba = \ba_m - \ba_b, \qquad \bomega = \bomega_m - \bomega_b .
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Nominal integration.} With gravity $\bg = (0,0,-\SI{9.80665}{\metre\per\second\squared})$,
\begin{align}
  \bp &\leftarrow \bp + \bv\,\Delta t + \tfrac{1}{2}(\R\ba + \bg)\,\Delta t^2, \\
  \bv &\leftarrow \bv + (\R\ba + \bg)\,\Delta t, \\
  \bq &\leftarrow \bq \otimes \exp\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\bomega\,\Delta t\right),
\end{align}
with the quaternion renormalized after each step; the biases are constant under nominal
integration.

\paragraph{Error-state transition.} Linearizing the local error dynamics gives
$\dx \leftarrow \bF\,\dx$ with the block matrix
\begin{equation}
  \bF =
  \begin{bmatrix}
    \I & \I\,\Delta t & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} \\
    \mathbf{0} & \I & -\R\skewm{\ba}\,\Delta t & -\R\,\Delta t & \mathbf{0} \\
    \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \exp(-\skewm{\bomega}\,\Delta t) & \mathbf{0} & -\I\,\Delta t \\
    \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \I & \mathbf{0} \\
    \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \I
  \end{bmatrix},
  \label{eq:F}
\end{equation}
in which the velocity-error coupling $-\R\skewm{\ba}$ is the first-order effect of an orientation
error on the rotated specific force, and the orientation block $\exp(-\skewm{\bomega}\Delta t)$ is
the exact rotation integral (evaluated via Rodrigues' formula) rather than its first-order
truncation~\cite{sola2017}.

\paragraph{Process noise.} The covariance propagates as $\bP \leftarrow \bF\bP\bF^{\!\top} + \bQ$
with the block-diagonal
\begin{equation}
  \bQ = \operatorname{diag}\!\big(\mathbf{0},\ \sigma_a^2\Delta t^2\,\I,\ \sigma_g^2\Delta t^2\,\I,\
  \sigma_{ab}^2\Delta t\,\I,\ \sigma_{\omega b}^2\Delta t\,\I\big).
\end{equation}
The IMU white-noise terms scale as $\Delta t^2$: a per-sample noise of standard deviation $\sigma$
perturbs the integrated velocity or orientation by $\sigma\Delta t$ over the step, injecting a
variance $\sigma^2\Delta t^2$ (this is also the only dimensionally consistent choice---$\sigma^2\Delta t$
would carry units of $\mathrm{m^2/s^3}$, not $\mathrm{(m/s)^2}$). The bias random walks scale as
$\Delta t$. A mis-scaled $\bQ$ is invisible in the RMSE but glaring in the NEES of
Section~\ref{sec:nees}.

\section{Measurement Updates}

For a measurement $\bz$ with model $\bz = h(\bx) + \bm{\nu}$, $\bm{\nu}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\bm{R})$,
the Jacobian $\bH = \partial h / \partial \dx$ is evaluated at the nominal state and the correction is
the standard sequence
\begin{align}
  \bm{S} &= \bH\bP\bH^{\!\top} + \bm{R}, &
  \bK &= \bP\bH^{\!\top}\bm{S}^{-1}, &
  \dx &= \bK\big(\bz - h(\bx)\big),
  \label{eq:kalman}
\end{align}
followed by injection (Section~\ref{sec:inject}) and the Joseph-form covariance update
\begin{equation}
  \bP \leftarrow (\I - \bK\bH)\,\bP\,(\I - \bK\bH)^{\!\top} + \bK\bm{R}\bK^{\!\top},
  \label{eq:joseph}
\end{equation}
which preserves symmetric positive-definiteness under finite precision where the shorter
$(\I-\bK\bH)\bP$ form drifts. The six models, each with the non-zero blocks of $\bH$, are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{GPS} (position): $h(\bx) = \bp$, so $\partial h/\partial\delta\bp = \I_3$.
  \item \textbf{Barometer} (altitude): $h(\bx) = \bm{e}_3^{\top}\bp$, so
        $\partial h/\partial\delta\bp = \bm{e}_3^{\top}$.
  \item \textbf{Magnetometer}: the world reference field $\bm{m}$ resolved in the body frame,
        $h(\bx) = \R^{\!\top}\bm{m}$. Using \eqref{eq:localerror},
        $\partial h/\partial\dtheta = \skewm{\R^{\!\top}\bm{m}}$, giving heading observability from
        the full vector rather than a yaw pseudo-measurement.
  \item \textbf{LiDAR altimeter}: a downward laser range to flat ground $z=0$ along the tilted body
        axis, $h(\bx) = p_z / R_{33}$, where $R_{33} = \bm{e}_3^{\top}\R\,\bm{e}_3$. Both
        $\partial h/\partial p_z = 1/R_{33}$ and
        $\partial h/\partial\dtheta = -\frac{p_z}{R_{33}^2}\,[-R_{32},\,R_{31},\,0]$ are non-zero, so a
        single beam senses height \emph{and} attitude.
  \item \textbf{UWB radio ranging}: scalar range to a fixed beacon $\bm{b}_k$ at a known position,
        $h(\bx) = \lVert\bp - \bm{b}_k\rVert$, with $\partial h/\partial\delta\bp = \hat{\bm{u}}^{\top}$,
        the unit line of sight. Being position-only, a few beacons localize the vehicle with no GPS
        at all~\cite{mueller2015}.
  \item \textbf{Optical flow}: the body-frame horizontal velocity $h(\bx) = \Pi\,\R^{\!\top}\bv$,
        with $\Pi$ selecting the first two components, so $\partial h/\partial\delta\bv = \Pi\R^{\!\top}$
        and $\partial h/\partial\dtheta = \Pi\skewm{\R^{\!\top}\bv}$; it observes velocity and
        attitude~\cite{honegger2013}.
\end{itemize}

Every Jacobian above is checked in the test suite against a central finite difference of its
measurement function across all $15$ error directions, agreeing to $10^{-4}$.

\section{Injection and Reset}
\label{sec:inject}

The error-state correction is folded into the nominal state and the error reset to zero:
\begin{equation}
  \bp \leftarrow \bp + \delta\bp,\quad
  \bv \leftarrow \bv + \delta\bv,\quad
  \bq \leftarrow \bq\otimes\exp\!\left(\tfrac12\dtheta\right),\quad
  \ba_b \leftarrow \ba_b + \delta\ba_b,\quad
  \bomega_b \leftarrow \bomega_b + \delta\bomega_b .
\end{equation}
Because the linearization point of the orientation error moves onto the new nominal quaternion, the
covariance is transformed by the reset Jacobian
\begin{equation}
  \bP \leftarrow \bm{G}\,\bP\,\bm{G}^{\!\top},\qquad
  \bm{G} = \operatorname{diag}\!\big(\I,\I,\ \I-\tfrac12\skewm{\dtheta},\ \I,\I\big).
\end{equation}
The term $\I-\tfrac12\skewm{\dtheta}$ is close to identity and is frequently omitted; retaining it is
one of the small details the consistency test rewards.

\section{Consistency: The NEES Gate}
\label{sec:nees}

An estimator that tracks well but reports the wrong uncertainty is not merely inelegant---it will
mislead whatever consumes its covariance. The decisive test is \emph{statistical consistency}: the
normalized estimation error squared~\cite{barshalom2001}
\begin{equation}
  \varepsilon = \bm{e}^{\top}\bP^{-1}\bm{e},
\end{equation}
where $\bm{e}$ is the estimation error expressed in the same $15$-dimensional tangent space that
$\bP$ describes (position and velocity differences, the body-frame logarithmic map for orientation,
and bias differences). For a consistent filter, $\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon] = \dim$. Averaged over $N$
independent Monte-Carlo runs, $N\bar\varepsilon \sim \chi^2(N\cdot\dim)$, which yields the two-sided
$95\%$ acceptance interval
\begin{equation}
  \bar\varepsilon \in
  \left[\ \frac{\chi^2_{0.025}(N\cdot\dim)}{N},\ \frac{\chi^2_{0.975}(N\cdot\dim)}{N}\ \right].
  \label{eq:neesband}
\end{equation}
A filter that is overconfident (covariance too small) lands above the band; one that is conservative
lands below. The runs are seeded from a realistic initial error drawn from the initial covariance, so
the test scores the filter and not an omniscient initialization.

\section{Simulation Environment}

Ground truth is an analytic banking helical trajectory---a horizontal orbit of radius \SI{40}{\metre}
and period \SI{30}{\second} with a superimposed vertical oscillation---so position, velocity, and
acceleration are exact and no integration error contaminates the reference. Orientation follows a
coordinated turn (nose along velocity, rolled into the turn by the bank angle that balances lateral
acceleration against gravity), and the body angular rate is obtained by central-differencing the
quaternion path. From this truth a strapdown IMU (specific force and body rate, with bias random
walk and white noise), and lower-rate GPS, barometer, magnetometer, LiDAR, UWB, and optical-flow
measurements are synthesized. Every noise level, bias-walk density, and per-sensor availability is a
parameter, so degradation and recovery can be driven interactively.

\section{Results}

Table~\ref{tab:nees} reports the Monte-Carlo consistency check over $40$ flights of \SI{30}{\second}
with all seven sensors active. Both the position ($3$-DOF) and full-state ($15$-DOF) NEES fall
inside the $95\%$ band of \eqref{eq:neesband}: the filter's covariance matches its error. The
time-averaged RMSE over the same runs is \SI{0.080}{\metre} in position, \SI{0.042}{\metre\per\second}
in velocity, and \SI{0.222}{\degree} in attitude.

the inertial-navigation physics visible. With \emph{all} aiding removed, the filter dead-reckons on
the IMU alone and the position error grows without bound, reaching hundreds of metres over
\SI{30}{\second}---exactly the quadratic error growth that accelerometer and gyroscope bias predict
for an unaided strapdown system~\cite{groves2013}. Yet a single position-fixing modality restores
sub-metre accuracy: with GPS denied but UWB ranging enabled, the error collapses from
\SI{16.6}{\metre} to \SI{0.16}{\metre}, confirming that the radio-ranging model and its Jacobian are
correct and that the filter degrades and recovers gracefully.

\section{Conclusion}

We have presented a quaternion error-state Kalman filter fusing seven sensors for inertial
navigation, in the standard local-error formulation, with each measurement Jacobian derived and
finite-difference-verified. The emphasis throughout is that correctness is \emph{demonstrated}, not
asserted: the full estimation loop passes a Monte-Carlo NEES consistency gate, and the filter
reproduces the expected inertial-navigation behavior---unbounded dead-reckoning drift, and graceful
recovery from any single position aid, including GPS-denied UWB ranging. The complete system, from
the dependency-free numerical core to a real-time 3D visualization, is open source and runs in the
browser.
